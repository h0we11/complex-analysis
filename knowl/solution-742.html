Solution 11.8.5.1.

Let \(Y(s)\) denote the Laplace transform of \(y(t)\text{,}\) then using the substitutionsÂ (11.8.1) and (11.8.2) results in
\begin{equation} -s^2Y\,'(s) -2sY(s) +0+sY\,'(s) + Y(s) - Y(s) = 0\text{.}\tag{11.8.3} \end{equation}
This equation involves \(Y\,'(s)\) and can be written as a first-order linear differential equation:
\begin{equation} Y\,'(s) + \left(\frac{2}{s-1}\right)Y(s) = 0\text{.}\tag{11.8.4} \end{equation}
The integrating factor \(\rho\) for the differential equation is
\begin{equation*} \rho = \exp\left(\int \frac{2}{s-1}\,ds\right) = e^{2\ln (s-1)}=(s-1)^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
Multiplying Equation (11.8.4) by \(\rho\) produces
\begin{equation*} (s-1)^2Y\,'(s) +2(s-1) Y(s) = \frac{d}{ds}\big[(s-1)^2Y(s)\big] = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
Now we integrate the equation \(\frac{d}{ds}\big[(s-1)^2Y(s)\big] = 0\) with respect to \(s\) and the results is \((s-1)^2Y(s) =C\text{,}\) where \(C\) is the constant of integration. Hence the solution to Equation (11.8.3) is
\begin{equation*} Y(s) = \frac{C}{(s-1)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
The inverse of the transform \(Y(s)\) in equation is the desired solution
\begin{equation*} y(t) = Cte^t \end{equation*}
