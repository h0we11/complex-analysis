For many students, it makes sense that the first series in Equations (7.2.10) converges only on the interval \((-1,1)\) because \(\frac{1}{1-x^2}\) is undefined at the points \(x=\pm 1\text{.}\) It seems unclear as to why this should also be the case for the series representing \(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\text{,}\) since the real-valued function \(f(x) =\frac{1 }{1+x^2}\) is defined everywhere. The explanation, of course, comes from the complex domain. The complex function \(f(z) =\frac{1}{1+z^2}\) is not defined everywhere. In fact, the singularities of \(f\) are at the points \(\pm i\text{,}\) and the distance between them and the point \(\alpha =0\) equals 1.
