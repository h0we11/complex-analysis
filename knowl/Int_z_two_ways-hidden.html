Show that
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1}z\,dz = \int_{C_2}z\ dz=4+2i\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(C_1\) is the line segment from \(-1-i\) to \(3+i\) and \(C_2\) is the portion of the parabola \(x=y^2+2y\) joining \(-1-i\) to \(3+i\text{,}\) as indicated in Figure 6.2.15.
The line segment joining \((-1,-1)\) to \((3,1)\) is given by the slope intercept formula \(y=\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) which can be written as \(x=2y+1\text{.}\) If we choose the parametrization \(y=t\) and \(x=2t+1\text{,}\) we can write segment \(C_1\) as
\begin{equation*} C_1:z(t)=2t+1+it \text{ and } dz=(2+i)\,dt, \text{ for } -1 \le t \le 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
Along \(C_1\) we have \(f(z(t) )=2t+1+it\text{.}\) Applying Theorem 6.2.11 gives
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1}z\ dz=\int_{-1}^1(2t+1+it)(2+i)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}
We now multiply out the integrand and put it into its real and imaginary parts:
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1}z\ dz=\int_{-1}^1(3t+2)\,dt+i\int_{-1}^1(4t+1)\,dt=4+2i\text{.} \end{equation*}
Similarly, we can parametrize the portion of the parabola \(x=y^2+2y\) joining \((-1,-1)\) to \((3,1)\) by \(y=t\) and \(x=t^2+2t\) so that
\begin{equation*} C_2:z(t)=t^2+2t+it \text{ and } dz=(2t+2+i)\,dt, \text{ for } -1 \le t \le 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
Along \(C_2\) we have \(f(z(t) )=t^2+2t+it\text{.}\) Theorem 6.2.11 now gives
\begin{align*} \int_{C_2}z\ dz \amp = \int_{-1}^1(t^2+2t+it) (2t+2+i)\,dt\\ \amp = \int_{-1}^1(2t^3+6t^2+3t)\,dt+i\int_{-1}^1(3t^2+4t)\,dt=4+2i\text{.} \end{align*}
