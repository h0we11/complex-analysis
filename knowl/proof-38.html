Proof

(We give a proof for a standard region, which is a region that is bounded by a contour \(C\) tha can be expressed in the two forms \(C=C_1+C_2\) and \(C=C_3+C_4\text{.}\)) If \(R\) is a standard region, then there exist functions \(y=g_1(x)\text{,}\) and \(y=g_2(x)\text{,}\) for \(a \le x \le b\text{,}\) whose graphs form the lower and upper portions of \(C\text{,}\) respectively, as indicated in Figure 6.3.5. As \(C\) is positively oriented, these functions can be used to express \(C\) as the sum of two contours \(C_1\) and \(C_2\text{,}\) where
\begin{align*} {3} \amp C_1 : z_1(t) = t+ig_1(t), \amp \amp \text{ for } \amp \amp a \le t \le b, \text{ and }\\ \amp C_2 : z_2(t) = -t+ig_2(-t), \amp \amp \text{ for } \amp \amp -b \le t \le -a\text{.} \end{align*}
We now use the functions \(g_1(x)\) and \(g_2(x)\) to express the double integral of \(-P_y(x,y)\) over \(R\) as an iterated integral, first with respect to \(y\) and second with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} -\iint_RP_y(x,y)\,dx\,dy = -\int_a^b\left[\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}P_y(x,y)\,dy\right]dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
Computing the first iterated integral on the right side gives
\begin{equation*} -\iint_RP_y(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \int_a^bP\big(x,\,g_1(x)\big)\,dx - \int_a^bP\big(x,\,g_2(x)\big)\,dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
In the second integral on the right side of this equation we can use the change of variable \(x=-t\) to obtain
\begin{equation*} -\iint_RP_y(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \int_a^bP\big(x,\, g_1(x)\big)\,dx + \int_{-b}^{-a}P\big(-t,\,g_2(-t)\big)(-1)\,dt \end{equation*}
Interpreting the two integrals on the right side of this equation as contour integrals along \(C_1\) and \(C_2\text{,}\) respectively, gives
\begin{equation} -\iint\limits_RP_y(x,y)\,dx\,dy =\int_{C_1}P(x,y)\,dx + \int_{C_2}P(x,y)\,dx = \int_CP(x,y)\,dx\text{.}\tag{6.3.2} \end{equation}
To complete the proof, we rely on the fact that for a standard region there exist functions \(x=h_1(y)\) and \(x=h_2(y)\) for \(c \le y \le d\) whose graphs form the left and right portions of \(C\text{,}\) respectively, as indicated in Figure 6.3.6.
Because \(C\) has positive orientation, it can be expressed as the sum of two contours \(C_3\) and \(C_4\text{,}\) where
\begin{align*} {3} \amp C_3 : z_3(t) = h_1(-t) -it, \amp \amp \text{ for } \amp \amp -d \le t \le -c, \text{ and }\\ \amp C_4 : z_4(t) = h_2(t) +it, \amp \amp \text{ for } \amp \amp c \le t \le d\text{.} \end{align*}
Using the functions \(h_1(y)\) and \(h_2(y)\text{,}\) we express the double integral of \(Q_x(x.y)\) over \(R\) as an iterated integral:
\begin{equation*} \iint\limits_RQ_x(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \int_c^{d} \left[\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)}Q_x(x,y)\,dx\right] dy\text{.} \end{equation*}
A derivation similar to that which led to Equation (6.3.2) shows that
\begin{equation} \iint\limits_RQ_x(x\text{,} y)\,dx\,dy = \int_CQ(x,y)\,dy\text{.}\tag{6.3.3} \end{equation}
Adding Equations (6.3.2) and (6.3.3) gives Equation (6.3.1).
