Solving Equations (5.1.5) for \(x\) and \(y\) yields
\begin{equation} x=\ln \rho \ \ \text{ and } y=\phi +2n\pi\text{,}\tag{5.1.8} \end{equation}
where \(n\) is an integer. Thus for any complex number \(w \ne 0\text{,}\) there are infinitely many complex numbers \(z=x+iy\) such that \(w=e^z\text{.}\) From Equations (5.1.8), the numbers \(z\) are
\begin{align} z \amp = x+iy=\ln \rho +i(\phi +2n\pi )\notag\\ \amp = \ln |w| +i(\mathrm{Arg}\textit{ }w+2n\pi )\text{.}\tag{5.1.9} \end{align}
where \(n\) is an integer. Hence
\begin{equation*} \exp\big[|w| +i(\mathrm{Arg}\,w+2n\pi)\big] = w \end{equation*}
