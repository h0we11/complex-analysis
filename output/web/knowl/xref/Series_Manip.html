Theorem 7.2.11.

Let \(f\) and \(g\) have the power series representations
\begin{align*} f(z) \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(z-\alpha)^n, \text{ for } z \in D_{r_1}(\alpha);\\ g(z) \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}b_n(z-\alpha)^n\text, \text{ for } z \in D_{r_2}(\alpha)\text{.} \end{align*}
If \(r=\min \{r_1,r_2\}\) and \(\beta\) is any complex constant, then
\begin{align} \beta f(z) \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\beta a_n(z-\alpha)^n, \text{ for } z \in D_{r_1}(\alpha),\tag{7.2.11}\\ f(z) +g(z) \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(a_n+b_n) (z-\alpha)^n, \text{ for } z \in D_r(\alpha), \text{ and }\tag{7.2.12}\\ f(z) g(z) \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_n(z-\alpha)^n, \text{ for } z \in D_r(\alpha)\text{,}\tag{7.2.13} \end{align}
where
\begin{equation} c_n = \sum_{k=0}^na_kb_{n-k}\text{.}\tag{7.2.14} \end{equation}
Identity (7.2.13) is known as the Cauchy product of the series for \(f\) and \(g\text{.}\)
