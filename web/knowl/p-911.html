Proceeding as we did in Example 2.6, we get the inverse mapping of \(u+iv=w=f(z) =\frac{1}{z}\) as \(z=f^{-1}(w) =\frac{1}{w}\text{.}\) Then
\begin{align} u+iv=w\in B\amp \Longleftrightarrow f^{-1}(w) =z=x+iy\in A\notag\\ \amp \Longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{u+iv}=x+iy\in A\notag\\ \amp \Longleftrightarrow \frac{u}{u^2+v^2}+i\frac{-v}{u^2+v^2}=x+iy\in A\notag\\ \amp \Longleftrightarrow \frac{u}{u^2+v^2}=x\ge \frac{1}{2}\ \ \text{ and } \frac{-v}{u^2+v^2}=y\notag\\ \amp \Longleftrightarrow \frac{u}{u^2+v^2}\ge \frac{1}{2}\tag{2.5.3}\\ \amp \Longrightarrow u^2-2u+1+v^2\le 1\tag{2.5.4}\\ \amp \Longleftrightarrow (u-1)^2+(v-0)^2\le 1\text{,}\notag \end{align}
which describes the disk \(\overline{D}_1(0)\text{.}\) As the reciprocal transformation is one-to-one, preimages of the points in the disk \(\overline{D}_1(0)\) will lie in the right half-plane \(\mathrm{Re}(z) \ge \frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) FigureÂ 2.5.4 illustrates this result.
