Example 9.4.3.

Show that the transformation \(w=f(z) =\sin z\) is a one-to-one conformal mapping of the vertical strip \(|x| \lt \frac{\pi}{2}\) onto the \(w\) plane slit along the rays \(u \le -1, \; v=0\text{,}\) and \(u \ge 1, \; v=0\text{.}\)
Solution.
Because \(f\,'(z) = \cos z\ne 0\) for values of \(z\) satisfying the inequality \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \lt \mathrm{Re}(z) \lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) it follows that \(w = \sin z\) is a conformal mapping. Using Equation 5, we write
\begin{equation*} u+iv = \sin z = \sin x\cosh y+i\cos z\sinh y \end{equation*}
If \(|a| \lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) then the image of the vertical line \(x=a\) is the curve in the \(w\) plane given by the parametric equations
\begin{equation*} u = \sin a\cosh y, \text{ and } v = \cos a\sinh y\text{,} \end{equation*}
for \(-\infty \lt y \lt \infty\text{.}\) Next, we rewrite these equations as
\begin{equation*} \cosh y = \frac{u}{\sin a}, \text{ and } \sinh y = \frac{v}{\cos a} \end{equation*}
We now eliminate \(y\) from these equations by squaring and using the hyperbolic identity \(\cosh^2y-\sinh^2y=1\text{.}\) The result is the single equation
\begin{equation} \frac{u^2}{\sin^2a} - \frac{v^2}{\cos^2a} = 1\text{.}\tag{9.4.1} \end{equation}
The curve given by Equation (9.4.1) is identified as a hyperbola in the \(uv\) plane that has foci at the points \((\pm1,0)\text{.}\) Therefore the vertical line \(x=a\) is mapped one-to-one onto the branch of the hyperbola given by Equation (9.4.1) that passes through the point \((\sin a,0)\text{.}\) If   \(0\lt a\lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) then it is the right branch; if \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\lt a\lt 0\text{,}\) it is the left branch. The image of the \(y\)-axis, which is the line \(x=0\text{,}\) is the \(v\)-axis. The images of several vertical lines are shown in Figure 9.4.4(a).
The image of the horizontal segment \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \lt x \lt \frac{\pi}{2}, \; y=b\) is the curve in the \(w\) plane given by the parametric equations
\begin{equation*} u = \sin x\cosh b, \text{ and } v=\cos x\sinh b \end{equation*}
for \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\lt x\lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) We rewrite them as
\begin{equation*} \sin x = \frac{u}{\cosh b}, \text{ and } \cos x = \frac{v}{\sinh b} \end{equation*}
We now eliminate \(x\) from the equations by squaring and using the trigonometric identity \(\sin^2x+\cos^2x=1\text{.}\) The result is the single equation
\begin{equation} \frac{u^2}{\cosh^2b} + \frac{v^2}{\sinh^2b} = 1\text{.}\tag{9.4.2} \end{equation}
The curve given by Equation (9.4.2) is identified as an ellipse in the \(uv\) plane that passes through the points \((\pm\cosh b,0)\) and \((0,\pm\sinh b)\) and has foci at the points \((\pm1,0)\text{.}\) Therefore, if \(b>0\text{,}\) then \(v = \cos x\sinh b > 0\text{,}\) and the image of the horizontal segment is the portion of the ellipse given by Equation (9.4.2) that lies in the upper half-plane \(\mathrm{Im}(w) > 0\text{.}\) If \(b\lt 0\text{,}\) then it is the portion that lies in the lower half-plane. Figure 9.4.4(b) shows the images of several segments.
