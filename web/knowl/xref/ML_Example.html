Example 6.2.20.

Use Inequality (6.2.14) to show that
\begin{equation*} \left| \int_c\frac{1}{z^2+1}\,dz\right| \le \frac{1}{2\sqrt{5}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
where \(C\) is the straight-line segment from 2 to \(2+i\text{.}\)
Solution.
Here \(|z^2+1| = |z-i| \, |z+i|\text{,}\) and the terms \(|z-i|\) and \(|z+i|\) represent the distance from the point \(z\) to the points \(i\) and \(-i\text{,}\) respectively. Referring to FigureÂ 6.2.21 and using a geometric argument, we get
\begin{equation*} |z-i| \ge 2, \text{ and } |z+i| \ge \sqrt{5}, \text{ for } z \text{ on } C \end{equation*}
Thus we have
\begin{equation*} |f(z)| =\frac{1}{|z-i|\,|z+i|} \le \frac{1}{2\sqrt{5}} = M\text{.} \end{equation*}
Because \(L\text{,}\) the length of \(C\text{,}\) equals 1, and Inequality (6.2.14) implies that
\begin{equation*} \left| \int_C\frac{1}{z^2+1}\,dz \right| \le ML =\frac{1}{2\sqrt{5}} \end{equation*}
