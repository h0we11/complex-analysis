Solution 7.2.10.1.

Computing derivatives for \(f(z)\) would be an onerous task. Fortunately, we can make use of the trigonometric identity
\begin{equation*} \sin^3 z = \frac{3}{4}\sin z - \frac{1}{4}\sin 3z\text{.} \end{equation*}
Recall that the series for \(\sin z\) (valid for all \(z\)) is \(\sin z= \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\text{.}\) Using the identity for \(\sin ^3z\text{,}\) we obtain
\begin{align*} \sin ^3z \amp = \frac{3}{4}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n \frac{z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1) !}-\frac{1}{4}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{(3z)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\\ \amp = \frac{3}{4}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1) !}-\frac{3}{4}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{9^nz^{2n+1}}{(2n+1) !}\\ \amp = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{3(1-9^n)}{4(2n+1) !}z^{2n+1}\text{.} \end{align*}
By the uniqueness of power series, this last expression is the Maclaurin series for \(\sin ^3z\text{.}\)
