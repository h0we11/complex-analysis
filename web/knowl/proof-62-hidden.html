We leave the details of establishing Equations (7.2.11) and (7.2.12) for you to do as an exercise. To establish Equation (7.2.13), we note that the function \(h(z) =f(z)g(z)\) is analytic in \(D_r(\alpha)\text{.}\) Thus, for \(z \in D_r(\alpha)\text{,}\)
\begin{align*} h\,'(z) \amp = f(z) g\,'(z) +f\,'(z) g(z) ;\\ h\,''(z) \amp = f\,''(z)g(z) +2f\,'(z) g\,'(z)+f(z) g\,''(z)\text{.} \end{align*}
By mathematical induction, we can generalize the preceding pattern to the \(n\) th derivative, giving Leibniz’s formula for the derivative of a product of functions:
\begin{equation} h\,^{(n)}(z) = \sum_{k=0}^n\frac{n!}{k!(n-k) !}f\,^{(k)}(z) g\,^{(n-k)}(z)\text{.}\tag{7.2.15} \end{equation}
(We will ask you to show this result in an exercise.) By Theorem 4.4.7 we know that
\begin{equation*} \frac{f\,^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}=a_k, \text{ and } \frac{g\,^{(n-k)}(\alpha)}{(n-k)!}=b_{n-k}\text{,} \end{equation*}
so Equation (7.2.15) becomes
\begin{equation} \frac{h^{\,(n)}(\alpha)}{n!} = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f\,^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}\frac{g\,^{(n-k)}(\alpha)}{(n-k) !} = \sum_{k=0}^na_kb_{n-k}\text{.}\tag{7.2.16} \end{equation}
Now, according to Taylor’s theorem,
\begin{equation*} h(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{h^{(n)}(\alpha)}{n!}(z-\alpha)^n\text{.} \end{equation*}
Substituting Equation (7.2.16) into this equation gives Equation (7.2.13) because of the uniqueness of power series.
