Proof

If we can establish Equation (7.2.1), the uniform convergence on \(\overline{D}_r(\alpha)\) for \(0\lt r\lt R\) will follow immediately from Theorem 7.1.5 by equating the \(c_k\) of that theorem with \(\frac{f\,^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}\text{.}\)
Let \(z_0 \in D_{R}(\alpha)\) and let \(r\) designate the distance between \(z_0\) and \(\alpha\) so that \(|z_0-\alpha | =r\text{.}\) Note that \(0 \le r\lt R\) because \(z_0\) belongs to the open disk \(D_{R}(\alpha)\text{.}\) We choose \(\rho\) such that \(0 \le r\lt \rho \lt R\text{,}\) and let \(C=C_{\rho }^{+}(\alpha)\) be the positively oriented circle centered at \(\alpha\) with radius \(\rho\) as shown in Figure 7.2.4.
With \(C\) contained in \(G\text{,}\) we can use the Cauchy integral formula to get
\begin{equation*} f(z_0) =\frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_C\frac{1}{z-z_0}f(z)\,dz \end{equation*}
Replacing \(\frac{1}{z-z_0}\) in the integrand by its equivalent expression in Lemma 7.2.2 gives
\begin{align} f(z_0) \amp = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_C\left[\frac{1}{z-\alpha} + \frac{z_0-\alpha}{(z-\alpha)^2} + \cdots + \frac{(z_0-\alpha)^n}{(z-\alpha)^{n+1}} \right.\notag\\ \amp \qquad \qquad \qquad +\left.\frac{1}{z-z_0}\frac{(z_0-\alpha)^{n+1}}{(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\right]\,f(z)\,dz\notag\\ \amp =\frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_C\frac{f(z)}{z-\alpha}\,dz+\frac{z_0-\alpha}{2\pi i} \int_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-\alpha)^2}\,dz+\cdots\notag\\ \amp \qquad \qquad \qquad + \frac{(z_0-\alpha)^n}{2\pi i} \int_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\,dz\notag\\ \amp \qquad \qquad \qquad + \frac{(z_0-\alpha)^{n+1}}{2\pi i}\int_C \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\,dz\text{,}\tag{7.2.2} \end{align}
where \(n\) is a positive integer. We can put the last term in Equation (7.2.2) in the form
\begin{equation} E_n(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_C\frac{(z_0-\alpha)^{n+1}f(z)}{(z-z_0)(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\,dz\text{.}\tag{7.2.3} \end{equation}
Recall also by the Cauchy integral formulas that
\begin{equation*} \frac{2\pi i}{k!}f\,^{(k)}(\alpha) = \int_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-\alpha)^{k+1}}\,dz , \text{ for } k=0,\,1,\,2,\ldots\text{.} \end{equation*}
Using these last two identities reduces Equation (7.2.2) to
\begin{equation*} f(z_0) = \sum_{k=0}^n\frac{f\,^{(k)}(\alpha)}{k!}(z_0-\alpha)^k+E_n(z_0) \end{equation*}
The summation on the right-hand side of this last expression is the first \(n+1\) terms of the Taylor series. Verification of Equation (7.2.1) relies on our ability to show that we can make the remainder term, \(E_n(z_0)\text{,}\) as small as we please by making \(n\) sufficiently large. We will use the ML inequality (Theorem 6.2.19) to get a bound for \(|E_n(z_0)|\text{.}\) According to the constructions shown in Figure 7.2.4, we have
\begin{equation} |z_0-a| = r \text{ and } |z-\alpha| =\rho\text{.}\tag{7.2.4} \end{equation}
By Property (1.3.5) of Section 1.3, we also have
\begin{align} |z-z_0| \amp = |(z-\alpha)-(z_0-\alpha)|\notag\\ \amp \ge |z-\alpha| - |z_0-\alpha|\notag\\ \amp = \rho -r\text{.}\tag{7.2.5} \end{align}
If we set \(M=\max\limits_{z \in C}|f(z)|\text{,}\) Equations (7.2.4) and (7.2.5) allow us to conclude that, for all \(z \in C\text{,}\)
\begin{align} \left|\frac{(z_0-\alpha)^{n+1}f(z)}{(z-z_0)(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\right| \amp = \left|\frac{(z_0-\alpha)^{n+1}}{(z-\alpha)^{n+1}}\right| \left|\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)}\right|\notag\\ \amp \le \left(\frac{r}{\rho}\right)^{\!n+1}\left(\frac{1}{\rho -r}\right)M\text{.}\tag{7.2.6} \end{align}
The length of the circle \(C\) is \(2\pi \rho\text{,}\) so the ML inequality in conjunction with Equations (7.2.3) and (7.2.6) gives
\begin{equation} |E_n(z_0)| \le \frac{1}{2\pi}\left(\frac{r}{\rho}\right)^{\!{n+1}}\left(\frac{1}{\rho -r}\right)M(2\pi \rho)\text{.}\tag{7.2.7} \end{equation}
Because \(0 \le r \lt \rho \lt R\text{,}\) the fraction \(\frac{r}{\rho}\) is less than 1, so \((\frac{r}{\rho })^{n+1}\) (and hence the right side of Equation (7.2.7)) goes to zero as \(n\) goes to infinity. Thus, for any \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) we can find an integer \(N_{\varepsilon}\) such that \(|E_n(z_0)| \lt \varepsilon\) for \(n \ge N_{\varepsilon}\text{,}\) and this fact completes the proof.
